Student Exam Performance

In life certain attributes and traits of people often contributes to societies unconscious bias of them. This is particularly present in the schooling system, where a students chance success is often pre-determined simply based on who they are. It's this type of problem that really gets you thinking, can we actually predict a student's success only based on their attributes?

In this project I will be analyzing a dataset of different students' performance on three types of exams. The data was collected in 2017 on a group of grade 6 students across a schooling district. The area of this district is unspecified. This data set can be found on kaggle.com and includes over 1000 data entries. Each entry in the data set is 1 student and contains both categorical and quantitative data. The categorical data includes information about their gender, race, parent level of schooling, lunch, and test preparation. Where the quantitative data covers their scores on math, reading, and writing exams.

We are going to use this data to analyze how these different variables may effect students test scores. This data analysis works to evaluate the validity of bias based on student attributes, and explore what actions parents can be taking to increase their child's success. Note that for this dataset race/ethnicity is referred to as groupings, this means we will not be naming races or ethnicity in our report.

What Are We Testing?

This project's overall purpose is to dive deeper into the effects that a student's background and attributes has on their average exam scores. We will explore this overall theme through a series of smaller questions shown below:

  1. What is the average exam scores of students given their race/ethnicity? What group has the highest?
  2. What is the most common combination of attributes for a student, how many unique students exist in this dataset?
  3. Does a student's parents completing post-secondary education give them an advantage when taking exams?
  4. Is a student's lunch level influenced by their parents education level?
  5. Are we really able to predict a student's average exam score given their attributes?
  6. What attribute gives a student the most advantage? What about the least?

Hypothesis Testing #1 (Education vs. Average)

To this point in the project we have been exploring the data and drawing conclusions using very typical practices. In this section we will explore a hypothesis we presented in one of our questions and use A/B testing to reach a conclusion.

A null hypothesis can be described as the hypothesis that assumes we have no connection. The alternative hypothesis is the hypothesis that we are looking to prove, and it contradicts the null hypothesis. Here are our null and alternative hypotheses:

Null Hypothesis: In the population, the distribution of average exam scores of students is the same for students with parents who have completed up to a highschool diploma and those whose parents have completed some capacity of post-secondary education.

Alternative Hypothesis: In the population, the average exam scores of students whose parents have completed post-secondary education will be higher, on average, than the students whose parents have only completed up to a highschool diploma.

Data Prep

We need to first prep our data before conducting A/B testing.

To conduct A/B testing, we want no more than 2 variations of our variable. This means we will group some of our data together, to make the data more manageable. These groups will be: highschool_and_below, college_and_above.

def difference_of_means(table, group_label):
    reduced = table[['average', group_label]]
    means_table = reduced.groupby(group_label)['average'].mean()
    return means_table.iloc[1] - means_table.iloc[0]

However to ensure that this is not just by chance from 1 random shuffle, we will create a code that will repeat this process many times for a more accurate result. To do that we must create a function that combines the code we just used for the shuffling process.

In[]:
def one_simulated_difference_of_means():

    # Shuffle the 'parental_level_of_education' column without replacement
    shuffled_labels = students_simple['parental_level_of_education'].sample(frac=1, replace=False).reset_index(drop=True)

    # Create a DataFrame with the shuffled labels and 'average' column
    shuffled_table = pd.DataFrame({'average': students_simple['average'], 'Shuffled Label': shuffled_labels})

    # Calculate the difference of means using the shuffled table
    return difference_of_means(shuffled_table, 'Shuffled Label')

Permutation Test and Conclusion

Now that we have established how we will simulate the test statistic, we will repeat this many times to collect a more accurate sample.

In[]:
differences = []

repetitions = 5000
for i in range(repetitions):
    new_difference = one_simulated_difference_of_means()
    differences.append(new_difference)

differences = np.array(differences)

Here we are conducting the previous test 5000 times, and we will look at the distribution of these test results.

In[]:
plt.hist(differences, bins=50)
print('Observed Difference:', observed_differenceAB)

Observed Difference: -5.14

This is the distribution of the 5000 observed differences from the random permutation. The distribution takes a center around 0, which makes sense as our null hypothesis says that the groups should have no difference in average exam scores. We can see that our original observed difference was -5.14, and it does not show up anywhere on this graph. This means that our test statistic and our observed difference are inconsistant under the null hypothesis. In other words the conclusion of the test would be that the data supports our alternative hypothesis over the null. We can compute a p-value to show how the hypothesis is favored.

In[]:
empirical_p = np.count_nonzero(differences <= observed_differenceAB) / repetitions
empirical_p
Out[]:
0.0

P-value is another way of saying the percentage chance of something happening as extreme as our result. The empirical p-value we calculated is 0, meaning in all of our 5000 tests, 0 of them found a difference of means as large as -5.14, or that there is a 0% chance of getting a difference of that magnitude. Although the actual chance is not 0, this is our approximation. We say that the result is statistically significant if the p-value is less than 0.05. Meaning that here with a p-value of 0, we can reject our null hypothesis.

Recap

In this section we tested our hypothesis that: In the population, the average exam scores of students whose parents have completed post-secondary education will be higher, on average, than the students whose parents have only completed up to a highschool diploma. We did this by applying A/B testing using 2 groups of parental education. From this test we found that our observed statistic had a p-value of 0. In other words there is an approximately 0% chance that our statistic would happen if there was no corelation between the 2 variables. This means that our hypothesis can was true, and we can conclude that students who have parents with higher levels of education will, on average, score higher on their exams. This answers our third question. 3. Does a student's parents completing post-secondary education give them an advantage when taking exams? Yes, students with parents who have completed post-secondary schooling will score higher on average.

Null Hypothesis: In the population, the chance of if a student receives either a standard or free/reduced lunch is the same for students with parents who have completed up to a highschool diploma and those whose parents have completed some capacity of post-secondary education.

Creating a Prediction Model

This section of the project is going to focus on making a prediction model that can use a students attributes as inputs, and output a predicted average exam score. In this section we will answer both questions five and six.

Our data set is unique in the sense that we have only categorical data as inputs, however some of these categories have more than 2 options. The way that we can get around this is to calculate how each versions of these inputs will effect our students average exam scores. This is called multiple regression, where we find the impact of variables in the form of slopes, and we use those to calculate a predicted exam value. The benefits of this is that our categorical data will be easier to equate to outcomes, and this regression method will give us an actual exam score prediction, rather than a passing/failing classification.

We will start by splitting our dataset into a training and test set.

In[]:
train_average = train['average']
train_attributes = train.drop(columns=['average'])

def rmse(slopes, attributes, average):
    errors = []
    for i in range(len(average)):
        predicted = predict(slopes, attributes.iloc[i])
        actual = average.iloc[i]
        errors.append((predicted - actual) ** 2)
    return np.sqrt(np.mean(errors))

def rmse_train(slopes):
    return rmse(slopes, train_attributes, train_average)
